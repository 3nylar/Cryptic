Four words, and one big idea

Imagine passing a note in class. Anyone in between can read it — unless you scramble it first. That scrambling is all a cipher is. Here are the only words you need.

The secret that turns one into the other.

shift by 3

Cipher

move each letter

The one rule that runs the whole field

The enemy is allowed to know exactly how your cipher works. Only the key is secret.

Every code protecting your bank details is public and published. Hiding the method isn't security — it's a delay. This idea is over 140 years old, and it's why we can hand you a working code-breaker on this page.

Cipher one · around 50 BC

The Caesar cipher:
slide every letter

Pick a number — say 3 — and shift every letter that many places along the alphabet. A becomes D, B becomes E, and Z wraps back around to C. Julius Caesar really used this. To unscramble, you shift back the same 3.

  • The key is just a number, 0 to 25.
  • Same secret both ways — shift forward to hide, back to reveal.
  • Its fatal flaw: there are only 26 possible keys. A computer tries all of them before your finger leaves the key.

Caesar's real protection wasn't the maths — it was that most of his enemies couldn't read.

The keyword repeats under the message

Both A's in ATTACK become different letters — because they sit under different keyword letters. That's what breaks the easy attack.

Cipher two · 1553

The Vigenère cipher:
a moving shift

Caesar's weakness is that it shifts everything the same amount. Vigenère fixes that with a keyword. Write the keyword over and over beneath your message, and shift each letter by its own keyword letter instead of one fixed number.

  • The key is a word, like LEMON — easy to remember.
  • The same letter hides differently each time, so simple letter-counting fails.
  • For 300 years it was called le chiffre indéchiffrable — "the unbreakable cipher."

A 12-letter keyword has 95 quadrillion possibilities. Hold that thought.

The punchline

A giant keyspace is not the same as security

26¹² = 95,428,956,661,682,176 keys
but the real work is only 312

Because the keyword repeats, a Vigenère message is really just several Caesar ciphers stacked together. The attacker figures out the keyword's length, splits the message into that many simple pieces, and cracks each one on its own. Ninety-five quadrillion becomes three hundred and twelve.

1

Find the length

A statistic called the index of coincidence reveals how long the keyword is — English text has a fingerprint that survives scrambling.

2

Split it up

Once the length is known, the message falls apart into that many separate, simple Caesar ciphers.

3

Crack each piece

Each piece is solved by counting letters — E is the commonest in English, and that never changes. Every answer is one letter of the keyword.

Ciphertext only

We need nothing but the scrambled text — the weakest thing an attacker can be handed. Both ciphers still fall.

Known words

Guess one word — "the", a date, a greeting — and the key falls out. Real messages are full of predictable words.

Human keys

Keys are words people can remember, so a dictionary beats the giant keyspace. It's still how most passwords fall today.

A large keyspace with a repeating pattern inside it is not a large keyspace.
